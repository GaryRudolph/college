#### WPI → Ivy transfer — why this is structurally bad

The 3-2 cooperative programs exist precisely to avoid brutal regular-transfer admit rates. Comparing:

| Path | Effective admit rate | Predictability |
|---|---|---|
| Columbia 3-2 via Hobart | Competitive review | Moderate |
| RPI 3-2 via Hobart | High | High |
| WashU 3-3 via Hobart | ~90% if criteria met | Near-automatic |
| WPI → Cornell Engineering (straight transfer) | ~10% or less | Low |
| WPI → Columbia SEAS (straight transfer) | ~5–6% | Very low |
| Cornell Fall 2023 overall transfer | 12.6% (834/6,581) — engineering runs lower | Low |
| Columbia Fall 2023 overall transfer | 5.75% (187/3,254) — SEAS competitive within | Very low |

Cornell Engineering is **gated on specific prereqs** — missing any one (Calc I/II, calc-based Physics I with lab, Engineering Gen Chem with lab) disqualifies the application. Cornell allows admission at end of year 1 (sophomore standing) or end of year 2 (junior standing), March 15 deadline.

**Strategic heuristic:** The mixed path — WPI with Ivy transfer as the plan — combines the weaknesses of both and achieves the worst odds of each. If Ivy is the target, work the 3-2 pipeline via Hobart. If engineering-from-day-1 is the target, go to WPI and commit.

### 3j. Syracuse University (via 4+2 pathway) — confirmed partnership, MS-only

- **Role in Jack's plan:** **Third primary dual-degree / direct-admission path** (alongside Columbia 2a and Dartmouth 2b) after WashU 3:3 (2c) and RPI 3:2 (2d) were demoted to unconfirmed-partnership status (see [`hobart-unconfirmed-paths.md`](./hobart-unconfirmed-paths.md)). Syracuse is the **only confirmed graduate-engineering pathway** with a published HWS partnership.

#### Syracuse 4+2 partnership — structure

In a March 2026 announcement, Syracuse's College of Engineering and Computer Science (ECS) launched 4+2 master's pathway partnerships with six NY partner schools (HWS, Canisius, Marist, Mercy, Nazareth, Utica). Structure: complete a BA/BS in **science or mathematics** at Hobart (4 years), then enter Syracuse ECS for an MS in engineering (typically 24 months / ~30 credits).

- **Admission gate:** 3.2 cumulative Hobart GPA + faculty recommendation + science or math major → **direct admission** (similar to WashU's "near-automatic if criteria met" standard).
- **Bridge course:** required for students from non-engineering BAs (physics BA qualifies; bridge may be waived for certain well-prepared students).
- **12 MS programs available:** biomedical engineering, chemical engineering, mechanical engineering, civil engineering, environmental engineering, computer engineering, computer science, cybersecurity, among others.

#### Syracuse 4+2 — what it actually delivers

### IRA and Ivy League rowing eligibility (critical for all 3:2 and 2:1:1:1 paths)

The governing bodies here are **IRA (Intercollegiate Rowing Association)** for competition rules, and the **Ivy League** for conference-specific overlays at Columbia and Dartmouth. **The NCAA is not directly the governing body** for men's heavyweight or lightweight rowing at Columbia, Dartmouth, or Hobart — the NCAA manual is the IRA's adopted baseline, but all eligibility questions are decided under IRA bylaws and (for Ivy schools) conference policy.

#### IRA eligibility rules (apply at all member schools including Hobart, Columbia, Dartmouth)

- IRA Eligibility & Operating Bylaws (irarowing.com) state: "no specific mention of an amendment to an NCAA rule in the IRA's Eligibility & Operating Bylaws should always be interpreted as the IRA following the rule as it is written in the NCAA manual." So IRA adopts the NCAA framework by default.
- **4 total seasons of competition** across a 5-calendar-year clock from first full-time enrollment.
- Freshman-level competition counts as a season used, even though freshmen can only race in freshman/novice events or V4/Open-4 (not Varsity 8 or 2V8).
- IRA itself does not have a separate redshirt policy different from the NCAA baseline. In principle, a redshirt year is possible at a non-Ivy IRA school.
- IRA does not bar graduate-student competition. Grad students do appear on IRA rosters and IRA All-Academic lists at non-Ivy schools; Columbia's 2022 IRA All-Academic list included a GS-designation athlete on the heavyweight side during the 2021–22 COVID-waiver year.
- IRA governs competition: Eastern Sprints (EARC), Dad Vail, IRA National Championship. Hobart competes in IRA despite being an NCAA Division III school overall.

#### Ivy League conference overlay (Columbia, Dartmouth — NOT Hobart)

This is the layer that actually ends Jack's Ivy rowing at Y5. Ivy League policy is stricter than IRA and trumps it at Ivy schools:

1. **Graduate students are ineligible for varsity athletics.** Ivy League conference policy restricts athletic opportunities to undergraduates. The one-time COVID waiver for the 2021–22 season let that year's seniors race as grad students and was explicitly not renewed ("this change is a direct result of the pandemic and will not be available in future years"). Columbia's 2025–26 men's lightweight roster has no GS designations — confirmation the rule is currently enforced.
2. **No redshirts allowed.** If an athlete is enrolled and medically able during the season, that season counts as used regardless of whether he competes. This is an Ivy overlay, not an IRA rule — IRA/NCAA would otherwise permit redshirting.
3. **Four-year expectation.** Student-athletes are expected to use their 4 seasons within their first 4 undergraduate years. This is stricter than the IRA/NCAA 5-year clock.
4. Limited medical-hardship and leave-of-absence exceptions exist but require Ivy compliance office approval — not a general strategy.

Sources: IRA Eligibility & Operating Bylaws (irarowing.com, 2020 rev.); Cornell Big Red Athletics (Athletic Eligibility and Compliance, 2024); Yale Bulldogs Student-Athlete Handbook Compliance (2024); Daily Pennsylvanian and Harvard Crimson coverage of the 2021 COVID waiver and its subsequent non-renewal; Columbia 2025–26 men's LW roster (gocolumbialions.com) for current-state confirmation.

#### Why the distinction between IRA and Ivy matters for Jack

- If the constraint were IRA-only (i.e. Jack went from Hobart to a non-Ivy IRA school), **a Y4 redshirt + Y5 race plan would work** — he'd have 1 season of IRA eligibility remaining and the 5-year clock accommodates it.
- The reason the redshirt plan doesn't work is **not IRA**, it's the **Ivy League conference overlay**. Columbia and Dartmouth apply the undergrad-only and no-redshirt rules independently of IRA. So the eligibility ceiling at the Ivy destination is lower than what IRA alone would allow.
- A non-Ivy IRA landing spot (e.g., MIT, BU, Georgetown, Gonzaga, Mercyhurst, UPenn for heavyweight) would behave differently — Penn is Ivy so same constraint there; MIT/BU etc. follow NCAA-baseline IRA rules. Not relevant to this decision since the Ivy destinations are Columbia and Dartmouth, but worth flagging if the search ever opens up.

## 6. Military option — the wildcard

Added in response to Jack's parent's note that military is a real consideration (USNA this summer, OCS after Hobart). The military option interacts with every primary path rather than competing with them, but the right entry path depends on **what role Jack actually wants** inside the service. Post-service education benefits (GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon) also make several primary paths dramatically cheaper for the Jack-ends-up-wanting-an-MS case.

### 6.0. The prior question: what does Jack want out of military service?

Officer roles sort roughly into four buckets. The best entry path depends on which of these Jack finds compelling.

#### Bucket 1 — Technical / nuclear

- **Roles:** Submarine Officer (designator 1170), SWO-Nuclear (1160), Naval Reactors Engineer (100B).
- **Day-to-day:** Running a nuclear propulsion plant (sub or carrier) or doing reactor engineering at Naval Reactors HQ / prototype sites. Long sea tours for sub/SWO-N (months underwater for subs); HQ engineering work for NR.
- **Culture:** Small peer group, demanding technical standards, prestige track, "nukes are different" identity inside the Navy.
- **Engineering fit:** Most technical path. Nuclear training itself is effectively a graduate-level engineering course.
- **Best entry:** NUPOC (junior/senior year) or USNA.
- **Degree fit:** Physics BA qualifies. ABET engineering BS also qualifies.
- **Signature compensation:** $5K+/month while still in college, $30K–$38K signing bonus, up to ~$168K before commissioning.
- **Service obligation:** 5 years active + 3 reserve.

#### Bucket 2 — Operational line

- **Roles:** Surface Warfare Officer (SWO, 1160), Naval Aviator (pilot), Naval Flight Officer (NFO), Marine Corps officer (ground or air).
- **Day-to-day:** Leading sailors or Marines, standing watch on a ship, flying aircraft, operational planning, deployments. More "leading people and running operations" than "doing engineering."
- **Culture:** Surface community has lower retention (33% remain after 10 years) and higher separation rates than the nuclear or aviation communities. Aviation and USMC have strong identity cultures.
- **Engineering fit:** Engineering degree helpful but not required. Day-to-day is leadership-and-operations, not technical engineering.
- **Best entry:** USNA or post-grad OCS (ROTC excluded on capacity grounds — see 6c).
- **Degree fit:** Any bachelor's qualifies.
- **Service obligation:** 4 years active (SWO), 8+ years for pilot/NFO due to training pipeline.

#### Bucket 3 — Engineering duty (closest to civilian engineering)

- **Roles:** Engineering Duty Officer (EDO, 1460), Civil Engineer Corps (CEC, 5100), Army Engineer Officer.
- **Day-to-day:**
    - **EDO:** Ship design, ship acquisition programs, maintenance engineering. Moves into program management for naval systems.
    - **CEC:** Public works, base infrastructure, NAVFAC construction projects. Think Bechtel / AECOM work but in uniform. Often co-leads with Seabees.
    - **Army Engineer:** Combat engineering, military construction, infrastructure, USACE civil works projects.
- **Culture:** Technical specialists inside the Navy/Army. Career-long engineering identity rather than rotating through command tracks.
- **Engineering fit:** **Closest to practicing civilian engineering inside the service.** Most direct transfer to civilian engineering careers after.
- **Best entry:**
    - **CEC:** OCS direct accession with ABET BS (or collegiate program with 18 months until graduation).
    - **EDO:** SWO → EDO redesignation (start as SWO, transition after qualifying).
    - **Army Engineer:** OCS, USMA, or **Direct Commissioning** (post-grad, requires ABET BS + 3 years relevant work experience). ROTC excluded on capacity grounds — see 6c.
- **Degree fit:** **Requires ABET-EAC engineering BS** (or architecture NAAB) for engineering-specific designators. **Hobart Applied Physics does NOT qualify for EDO, CEC, or Army Engineer Direct Commission.**
- **Service obligation:** 4 years active + 4 reserve (CEC); 8 years total for Army Direct Commission.

#### Bucket 4 — Special operations / combat arms

- **Roles:** Navy SEAL, EOD, Marine infantry, Army Ranger / Special Forces, combat engineer.
- **Day-to-day:** Physical-first, high-commitment, small-unit combat and specialized operations.
- **Culture:** Selection-based identity; SpecOps communities are tight.
- **Engineering fit:** Engineering not central.
- **Best entry:** USNA, post-grad OCS, or enlisted-to-officer tracks (ROTC excluded on capacity grounds — see 6c).
- **Degree fit:** Any bachelor's qualifies.

#### Key conversation to have with Jack

Which bucket appeals to him? The answer significantly narrows the entry-path decision:

- **Technical / nuclear** → NUPOC is the primary path; USNA works if he also wants the Academy experience.
- **Operational line** → OCS or USNA; engineering degree not required.
- **Engineering duty** → OCS with ABET BS (CEC direct accession); **Option 2f qualifies only after completing the state-school BSME, not after the Hobart physics BA alone.**
- **SpecOps / combat** → any entry path; physical performance dominates.

### 6.1. Can military service pay for a BS or MS in engineering afterward? Yes — three mechanisms.

This is directly relevant to the decision because it meaningfully changes the effective cost math for Option 2f (Hobart BA → State School BSME), Option 1 (WPI), and Option 3 (UBC), and is relevant for anyone who enters the service without the degree they ultimately want.

#### (i) Post-9/11 GI Bill (Chapter 33) — the primary benefit

- **Earned:** 100% benefit after 36 months (3 years) of active duty. Sliding scale from 40% at 90 days to 100% at 36+ months.
- **Tuition coverage (2025–26 rates):**
    - **Public schools (in-state):** 100% of net tuition and mandatory fees.
    - **Private schools:** Capped at **$29,920.95/year** for net tuition and mandatory fees.
    - **Foreign schools:** Capped at same amount.
- **Housing allowance:** Monthly BAH at the E-5 with-dependents rate for the school's ZIP code (can be $2K–$4K+/month in high-cost areas).
- **Books stipend:** $1,000/year.
- **Duration:** 36 months of full-time benefits (effectively a 4-academic-year bachelor's, or 18–24 months of grad school).
- **Transfer to dependents:** Allowed after 6 years of service with an additional 4-year commitment.

#### (ii) Yellow Ribbon Program — private-school top-up

Yellow Ribbon is a dollar-for-dollar match between the school and the VA for tuition above the GI Bill cap. Requires 100% GI Bill eligibility.

| School | Yellow Ribbon contribution | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Dartmouth** | **Uncapped, full remaining tuition** | No limit on participants. A+ rating. Covers Thayer School of Engineering fully. |
| **Cornell** | Full remaining tuition | Capped at ~100 veterans on campus; first-come-first-served for grad students. Covers Cornell Engineering. |
| **Columbia** | $8,000/year additional | Most veterans enter via General Studies. Grad (SEAS) Yellow Ribbon participation varies — verify before applying. |
| **Stanford** (reference) | $16K–$20K/year for engineering grad | Uncapped. |
| **WashU** | Offers Yellow Ribbon; varies by school | Graduate engineering participation worth checking. |
| **WPI** | Offers Yellow Ribbon | Covers bulk of tuition for veterans. |
| **Ivies generally** | Most Ivies offer Yellow Ribbon with various caps | Worth checking per-school. |

**Practical implication:** a veteran with 100% GI Bill eligibility can attend **Dartmouth Thayer Engineering, Cornell Engineering, or Stanford Engineering for near-zero tuition** post-service. The Columbia gap is larger ($8K YR + $30K GI Bill vs. $70K tuition ≈ $32K/year uncovered), so Columbia is a weaker fit for the post-service MS path.

#### (iii) Navy-funded graduate education (active duty)

Available *during* service, before separation, for selected officers.

- **Fleet Scholars Education Program (FSEP):** Navy pays full tuition and the officer continues drawing salary for up to 24 months at any accredited U.S. grad school (MIT, Stanford, Purdue, Michigan, etc.) for a master's. Competitive selection. Typically O-3 / O-4 ranks, strong records. Distance learning and executive programs not eligible.
- **CIVINS (Civilian Institutions program):** Umbrella that includes FSEP. 71 subspecialties, 33 fields of study, 100+ participating civilian institutions. ~22% of graduate officer assignments annually go to civilian institutions.
- **Naval Postgraduate School (NPS):** Monterey, CA. Navy-internal grad school, tuition-free, full pay. Offers master's degrees in engineering, operations research, cybersecurity, etc. Standard path for officers seeking a master's while continuing to serve.
- **Tuition Assistance (TA):** $4,500/year cap, $250/semester hour. Useful for part-time coursework, not a full-degree funder.

#### Dollar value of these benefits

Rough estimates of tuition-only value if Jack completes 3+ years of active duty and goes to grad school after:

| Grad program | GI Bill + YR covers | Out-of-pocket tuition |
|---|---|---|
| Dartmouth Thayer (MEng/MS) | $30K + uncapped YR | **~$0** |
| Cornell Engineering (MEng/MS) | $30K + full YR (if slot available) | ~$0 |
| Stanford Engineering MS | $30K + $16K–$20K YR | ~$20K–$25K/year residual |
| Columbia SEAS (MS) | $30K + $8K YR | ~$32K/year residual |
| WashU McKelvey (MS) | $30K + YR (varies) | Varies, likely low |
| State school (in-state MS) | 100% | $0 |

**Total tuition value of the benefit across a BS or MS:** **$120K at a public school; $200K–$300K+ at an elite private engineering program** — before counting the BAH stipend and salary during FSEP/CIVINS.

#### How this changes the cost math for the primary paths

| Primary path | Stand-alone all-in cost | With military service + GI Bill grad school after |
|---|---|---|
| Option 1 (WPI) | ~$220K | Can add Dartmouth/Cornell/Stanford MS for ~$0 post-service. Strong cost efficiency. |
| Option 2g (Hobart BA → OCS / NUPOC, skip the state-school BSME) | ~$184K (Hobart BA only) | Can add **Dartmouth Thayer MS for ~$0** post-service. End state: Physics BA + Navy nuclear service + Dartmouth MEng. |
| Option 3 (UBC) | ~$268K (USD) | Same — post-service MS at Dartmouth/Cornell possible. Upgrades the US-brand-recognition problem of the UBC undergrad. |
| Options 2a/2b (Hobart → Ivy) | ~$275K (2b) / ~$304K (2a) | Less value-add since Ivy BS is already in hand; still available if he wants a further MS. |

**Key insight:** the military path is not just "how Jack pays for his first degree" — it's also a **structured path to an elite engineering MS at near-zero tuition after service.** This is particularly powerful for Options 1 and 2g, where the undergrad credential is less prestigious (or less expensive) than an Ivy BS and can be topped with a Dartmouth/Cornell/Stanford MS for effectively free.

### 6a. USNA (US Naval Academy) — apply this summer

#### Eligibility

- U.S. citizen by July 1 of year of entry.
- At least 17 years old and **not having passed 23rd birthday by July 1 of entry year**.
- Unmarried with no dependents.
- Valid SSN.

#### Application process (same for HS students and college students)

1. Submit preliminary application (open to current college students).
2. **Obtain a nomination** — from a U.S. Senator, Representative, Vice President, or via military-affiliated categories (Presidential, ROTC, enlisted, etc.).
3. Complete the full candidate application.
4. Pass medical examination (DoDMERB).
5. Pass Candidate Fitness Assessment.
6. Complete interview with a Blue and Gold Officer.

#### Timing

- Applications typically open **April** with **January 31 deadline** for the following academic year.
- Jack would be applying in summer/fall 2026 for Class of 2031 (entering July 2027).

#### Critical constraints for an already-enrolled college student

- **USNA does not accept transfer credit.** All admits — regardless of prior college — **start over as plebes and complete the full 4-year program.**
- This means if Jack enrolls at Hobart in fall 2026 and then accepts USNA for fall 2027, his Hobart freshman year yields no academic credit at USNA. He's Class of 2031 instead of 2030.
- Acceptance rate: ~8% of applicants. With nomination hurdle on top.

#### Engineering at USNA

- USNA is an **ABET-accredited engineering school.**
- ~60% of midshipmen major in STEM (engineering, science, math).
- Majors include Aerospace, Mechanical, Electrical, Naval Architecture, Systems Engineering, Ocean Engineering, Nuclear Engineering, and more.
- **Delivers a BS in Engineering with no tuition cost.**

#### Rowing at USNA

- Navy has strong **heavyweight** rowing (Patriot League DI).
- **Lightweight program exists**, smaller roster. Navy LW competes at Eastern Sprints.
- Competitive racing at the DI level.

#### Post-USNA commitment

- Commissioned as an Ensign (O-1) upon graduation.
- **5 years active duty + 3 years reserve** minimum. Longer for Navy pilot/NFO (8 years), submarine nuclear (5 years), etc.

### 6b. OCS / NUPOC after any bachelor's degree

Relevant as an **end-state officer pipeline** regardless of which civilian college Jack attends.

#### Navy OCS — general

- 13-week program at Officer Training Command Newport, RI.
- Age 19–36 at commissioning (waivers to 42).
- U.S. citizen.
- Officer Aptitude Rating (OAR) exam; scores above 45 preferred (waivers to 40 possible).
- **Minimum 2.7 GPA** to be considered.

#### OCS designators and degree requirements

- **Engineering Duty Officer (EDO):** Requires **ABET-EAC accredited engineering degree** (Engineering Accreditation Commission). ABET-ETAC degrees may work with EIT/PE credentials or waivers.
  - **Hobart Applied Physics BA does NOT qualify for EDO.**
  - Columbia/WashU/RPI/WPI/UBC engineering degrees **do qualify** (ABET or CEAB).
- **Unrestricted Line (SWO, aviation, NFO, intel, supply, submarine, etc.):** Requires a **bachelor's in any field.**
  - **Physics is perfectly fine.**
  - Pilot and NFO programs do not require engineering specifically.
- **Nuclear Propulsion Officer Candidate (NUPOC):** Specialized pipeline for nuclear surface, submarine, or Naval Reactors roles.

#### NUPOC — why this matters for the Hobart Applied Physics path

- **Explicitly open to physics majors** with strong technical coursework.
- Eligibility windows:
  - Within 42 months of graduating with a bachelor's degree
  - Within 54 months of completing a combined bachelor's + master's
  - Currently in a master's program within 12 months of earning it
  - Already possess a bachelor's degree
- Degrees must come from **regionally accredited U.S. schools.** (UBC as a Canadian school may not qualify — requires confirmation with recruiter.)
- **Academic standards:** Minimum C in technical coursework; any below-C requires a waiver. Competitive applicants aim for A's and B's.
- **GPA rules of thumb:**
  - Submarine / Surface Warfare applicants: **3.0+**
  - Instructor applicants: **3.4+**
  - Naval Reactors Engineer applicants: **3.7+** (typically for technical majors)
- **Technical coursework expected:** calculus (through DiffEq), calc-based physics, often chemistry. **Hobart Applied Physics covers all of this.**
- **Compensation:** signing bonuses $15K–$38K common; pay during school; officer salary post-commission.
- **Service obligation:** 5–8 years depending on community.
- Must qualify for **secret clearance** (top secret for Submarine and Naval Reactors).

#### Why this changes the calculus on Option 2f

If Jack ends up with only a Hobart Applied Physics BA (because the 3:2 didn't work out and he decided not to spend 2 more years chasing an ABET BSME at a state school), **NUPOC is a legitimate elite officer pipeline** that doesn't require an engineering BS. That turns Option 2f from "failed engineering path" into "physics undergrad → nuclear Navy officer" — making the state-school BSME leg optional rather than required.

### 6c. ROTC — considered and excluded

NROTC / AROTC / AFROTC scholarships can cover full tuition + stipend at participating civilian schools in exchange for a post-grad service commitment. On paper this is attractive for the cost profile of Options 3 and 4.

**However, ROTC is not on the table for Jack because of capacity.** ROTC requires:

- Pre-dawn PT ~3 days/week (0500–0600 typical)
- Weekly military-science class plus evening drill/lab
- Multi-week summer training commitments (ex: NROTC CORTRAMID after sophomore year, cruise blocks after junior year)
- Physical readiness testing and uniform standards year-round

Layered on top of:

- An engineering curriculum (15–18 credits/semester including labs and design sequences)
- A serious rowing program (practice 6x/week, racing season travel, winter erg volume)

**The aggregate load is not compatible with maintaining the GPA needed for 3:2 admission (3.30–3.50) or with performing in a competitive boat.** Something would give — either grades, rowing, or ROTC performance — and any of the three being compromised defeats the point of the decision.

**The practical military entry paths for Jack are therefore:**

- **USNA** (freshman restart, no concurrent civilian-college load)
- **NUPOC** (junior/senior year application; no training load during college beyond application process)
- **Post-grad OCS** (after graduation, no college-period load at all)

ROTC is excluded from the decision matrix and from all cost/path recommendations going forward.

Parallel track — doesn't foreclose the 3:2 portfolio if rejected. **Important caveat:** USNA does not accept transfer credit; if admitted after Hobart freshman year, Jack restarts as a plebe (Class of 2031 instead of 2030).

### Post-grad OCS as the Hobart-BA pivot (Option 2g)

If the dual-degree forks fail (Columbia / Dartmouth / WashU / RPI all reject) and Jack still wants an elite engineering credential, **post-grad OCS + GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon** is materially better than the Cal State BSME (Option 2f) for the final credential. ### Pivot rules

- **GPA checkpoint end of sophomore year:** if cumulative < 3.25, have a serious conversation about direct transfer to an engineering school vs. another year of Hobart tuition.

(b) Finish Hobart BA + Cal State BSME (Option 2f, ~$242K / 6 yrs) — keeps the BA but adds ~$46K. (d) Finish Hobart BA + Syracuse ECS 4+2 MS (Option 2e, ~$289K / 6 yrs) — keeps things civilian and near-automatic at 3.2 GPA, but delivers an MS without an ABET BS, which is a weaker credential profile than (a) or (b). Ranking by credential quality: (c) > (b) ≈ (a) > (d). Pick (a) or (b) for ABET BS cleanliness, (c) if military service is independently attractive, (d) only if both (a)/(b) also fall through.
