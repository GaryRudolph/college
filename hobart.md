# If Hobart: the junior-year fork

*Follow-on to [`README.md`](./README.md). Read this only if the starting-school choice is Hobart — otherwise the content here doesn't apply. For the full research base, see [`background.md`](./background.md).*

## The sub-decision

If the starting-school answer is Hobart, the next question is which dual-degree partner(s) to target at the junior-year fork (sophomore year for Dartmouth). The portfolio strategy:

> **Apply to Dartmouth sophomore year, Columbia junior year, then Syracuse ECS direct-admission senior year, with Cal State BSME as the end-state safety net.**

This gives Jack two distinct Ivy shots plus a near-automatic graduate-engineering pathway plus a near-automatic Cal State fallback, without requiring any single application to succeed.

---

## The three primary dual-degree / direct-admission paths

- **Option 2a — Hobart → Columbia 3:2 (~$304K all-in, 5 yrs, 4 rowing seasons, 1 at Columbia LW).** Elite Ivy engineering credential (SEAS BS). 3 Hobart IRA seasons + 1 Columbia LW season (Y4 only; Y5 Ivy-ineligible as grad). 3.50 GPA gate at junior-year fork. Cleaner rowing arc than Dartmouth — no Y4 interruption.
- **Option 2b — Hobart → Dartmouth 2:1:1:1 (~$316K all-in, 5 yrs, 3–4 rowing seasons, 1 at Dartmouth LW).** Elite Ivy engineering credential (Thayer BE). 2 Hobart seasons + 1 Dartmouth LW (Y3) + optional 1 Hobart openweight (Y4, burns his 4th season) + no Y5 rowing (Ivy-ineligible). **Slightly more expensive than 2a Columbia (~$304K)** — both Y3 and Y5 at Dartmouth are billed at full Dartmouth tuition; the Hobart $41K/yr merit scholarship does not transfer to the Y3 dual-degree year (resolved 2026-04-29 — see [`hobart-questions.md` §3.2](./hobart-questions.md#3-hobart-financial-aid)), so the prior "Y3 routed through Hobart at the scholarship rate" model is rejected. The 2b case rests on the **earlier sophomore-year deadline** (Feb 1 Y2, ~10 months ahead of Columbia's Feb 1 Y3), the **lower GPA gate** (~3.30 vs. 3.50), and the portfolio-strategy benefit of being able to fall back to Columbia after a Dartmouth rejection. Y4 rowing is Hobart openweight only, and Dartmouth Y3 grades appear on the transcript for all downstream fallback applications.
- **Option 2e — Hobart → Syracuse ECS 4+2 (~$289K all-in, 6 yrs).** Hobart BA (4 yrs) → Syracuse College of Engineering and Computer Science MS (2 yrs). **Direct-admission pathway announced by Syracuse ECS for 6 NY partner schools (HWS included) — 3.2 Hobart GPA + faculty recommendation + bridge course = near-automatic admit.** 12 MS fields available including ME, BME, civil, chemical, computer engineering, CS, and cybersecurity. **Lowest-risk of the three primary paths** — admit is near-automatic at a lower GPA gate than either Ivy. Rowing: 4 Hobart IRA seasons, ends at graduation (Syracuse crew is heavyweight-only IRA, and grad-student eligibility is exhausted under the 4-in-5 clock — same as 2f and 2g below). **Caveat — credential profile:** MS-without-ABET-BS is weaker than 2f (BA + Cal State ABET BSME) for early-career engineering roles and PE licensure, and Syracuse ECS (~#70) ranks below Columbia / Dartmouth and below the WPI BSME (~#64). Compare against 2g (Hobart BA + post-service MS at Dartmouth Thayer / Cornell / Stanford) for a similar end-state at lower out-of-pocket cost, gated on military service. **2e is best framed as an insurance policy against a middling GPA, not a primary target** — see [`hobart-syracuse.md`](./hobart-syracuse.md) for the partnership-vs-open-market-MEng analysis (when 2e earns its place vs. when it's dominated by Cornell MEng / Dartmouth Thayer MEng / BU / Northeastern open-market shots at similar cost).

> **Note:** Two earlier-tracked options — **2c Hobart → WashU 3:3** and **2d Hobart → RPI 3:2** — were **definitively closed** by Prof. Donald Spector's 2026-04-24 email: "No, there is not a joint degree relationship with Wash U. or Rensselaer." HWS's 2024–25 catalogue ([Joint Degree Programs → Engineering](https://hws.edu/catalogue/curriculum.aspx)) correctly lists only Columbia and Dartmouth; WashU McKelvey's affiliate list excludes HWS; no RPI 3+2 partner list includes HWS. Legacy research on these paths lives in [`hobart-unconfirmed-paths.md`](./hobart-unconfirmed-paths.md) as a closed archive. The verbatim Spector reply is in [§1 of `hobart-questions.md`](./hobart-questions.md#1-prof-ileana-dumitriu--hws-engineering-liaison). Standard competitive graduate / transfer admission to WashU or RPI remains theoretically open to Jack but is not a Hobart-articulated pathway.

## The other Hobart forks

- **Option 2f — Hobart BA → Cal State BSME (~$242K all-in, 6 yrs).** The end-state safety net if every dual-degree fork fails. 4 years Hobart IRA rowing + Cal State BSME at CA resident tuition. Cheapest of the Hobart paths. Rowing ends at Hobart; Cal State crew is club-level. Low-moderate admit risk (CSU typically needs 2.7 GPA + prereqs). Also a natural entry point for NUPOC or unrestricted-line OCS — see [`military.md`](./military.md) for entry-path detail. *Note: if Jack commutes from home during the 2 Cal State years, this path drops by ~$40K (saves on R&B) to ~$202K.*
- **Option 2g — Hobart BA → OCS → post-service MS (~$184K undergrad + $0 grad, 4 yrs college then service).** Engineering reputation: **Solid to Elite** — Hobart BA alone is solid; the path unlocks a tuition-free MS in engineering at Dartmouth Thayer, Cornell, or Stanford after service, which lands the final credential in the elite tier. Rowing: same as 2f — 4 Hobart IRA seasons, ends at graduation. Cost: 4 years of Hobart at ~$46K/yr (tuition + R&B, with the $41K/yr merit scholarship) ≈ **~$184K out-of-pocket college cost**, then commissioned service pays the rest. **Civilian parallel:** 2e achieves a roughly similar end-state (Hobart BA + MS) without military service, but at higher out-of-pocket cost (~$289K vs. ~$184K) and with a weaker engineering MS credential (Syracuse #70 vs. Dartmouth Thayer / Cornell / Stanford). See the three mechanisms below.

### How Option 2g pays for the engineering credential

Three mechanisms stack to make a post-service MS effectively free at the right schools:

1. **Post-9/11 GI Bill (after 3 years of active duty, 100% benefit).** Pays **$29,920.95/year for private schools** (2025–26 rate; adjusts annually) and **100% of in-state tuition at public schools**. Plus a monthly housing allowance (~BAH E-5 with dependents for the school's ZIP code) and $1,000/year for books. Covers up to 36 months of full-time enrollment. **Transferable to a spouse or dependents after 6 years of service with a 4-year additional commitment.**
2. **Yellow Ribbon Program** (private-school top-up to the GI Bill).
  - **Dartmouth:** uncapped — covers full remaining tuition, unlimited participants (A+ rating).
  - **Cornell:** covers the remainder, capped at ~100 veterans on campus, first-come-first-served for grad students.
  - **Columbia:** ~$8,000/year additional. Most veterans enter via the General Studies program; grad school (SEAS, etc.) participation varies.
  - **Stanford** (for reference): $16K–$20K additional for graduate engineering, uncapped.
  - **Practical implication:** **a veteran can attend Dartmouth Thayer, Cornell Engineering, or Stanford Engineering for near-zero tuition** if they have 100% GI Bill eligibility.
3. **Navy-funded graduate education (active duty).** While still in service, officers can apply for:
  - **CIVINS / Fleet Scholars Education Program (FSEP):** Navy pays full tuition + salary for up to 24 months at any accredited U.S. grad school (MIT, Stanford, Purdue, etc.) for a master's. Competitive, typically O-3 / O-4 officers with strong records.
  - **Naval Postgraduate School (NPS):** Monterey, CA. Navy-internal grad school offering master's in engineering fields, tuition-free for selected officers. Another standard path.
  - **Tuition Assistance (TA):** $4,500/year cap — useful for part-time coursework, not a full degree funder.

**Net cost implication for Option 2g:**

- 4 years of Hobart BA (tuition + R&B at ~$46K/yr net of the $41K scholarship): ~$184K.
- Military service: paid (officer salary during service).
- Post-service MS: **$0 tuition** at Dartmouth Thayer via GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon (the most generous Ivy for veterans), or near-zero at Cornell / Stanford. Plus housing allowance and books stipend during school.
- Or active-duty master's: $0 tuition at Stanford / MIT / Purdue via CIVINS / FSEP, while being paid.
- **Total out-of-pocket all-in cost for a Hobart BA + elite engineering MS: ~$184K.** Total tuition value of the GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon benefits: **$120K–$300K+ depending on school and path**, before counting salary.

**What makes 2g distinct from 2a–2f:** the engineering credential is delayed by service time (typically 3–5 years) but ends up elite without needing the 3.50 GPA transfer gate (2a), sophomore-year Dartmouth review (2b), or 6 years of continuous college (2c, 2f). The trade is the military service commitment itself, not the credential quality.

**When 2g is the right play:** if Jack is already drawn to military service (especially nuclear / submarine via NUPOC, or Navy officer more broadly), and wants an elite engineering credential without an undergraduate transfer gamble. Also works as a **post-hoc pivot from 2f** — if every dual-degree fork fails and he lands at the Hobart BA, post-grad OCS + GI Bill Yellow Ribbon is materially better than a Cal State BSME for the final credential.

## How the Hobart BA closes across paths

The Hobart BA is the common credential across every 2x option (2a–2g), but the mechanism by which it reaches HWS's 32-course requirement differs per path — and that mechanism determines why Y4 at Hobart is structurally mandatory for 2e/2f/2g.

At the end of Hobart Y3, Jack has **24 of the 32 courses HWS requires for the BA** (per the shared core in [`hobart-plan.md`](./hobart-plan.md): `4 courses × 2 semesters × 3 years = 24`). He does **not** yet have a conferred BA. The remaining ~8 courses close as follows:

| #  | Option                        | How the 32-course BA closes                                                                                         | BA conferred                   | Total years            |
| -- | ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ | ---------------------- |
| 2a | Hobart → Columbia 3:2         | 24 at HWS (Y1–Y3) + Columbia Y4 courses transfer back to fill remaining ~8                                           | End of Columbia Y1 (Y4)        | 5                      |
| 2b | Hobart → Dartmouth 2:1:1:1    | Y1–Y2 at HWS + Y3 at Dartmouth (full Dartmouth tuition; credits transfer back to HWS for BA) + Y4 at HWS finishing physics major | End of Y4                      | 5                      |
| 2e | Hobart → Syracuse ECS 4+2     | **4 full years at HWS**; 32 courses self-completed                                                                   | End of Y4                      | 6 (4 BA + 2 MS)        |
| 2f | Hobart BA → Cal State BSME    | **4 full years at HWS**; 32 courses self-completed                                                                   | End of Y4                      | 6 (4 BA + 2 CSU)       |
| 2g | Hobart BA → OCS → post-svc MS | **4 full years at HWS**; 32 courses self-completed                                                                   | End of Y4                      | 4 + service + MS       |

**Y4 at Hobart is the universal fallback year.** If Jack reaches the junior-year fork and Columbia rejects (Feb Y3) and Dartmouth had already rejected (Feb Y2), the default next year is Hobart Y4. It self-completes the BA, covers his 4th and final IRA rowing season (see [Critical eligibility note in `README.md`](./README.md#critical-eligibility-note-why-the-5--and-6-year-paths-do-not-mean-56-years-of-racing)), and is the structural prerequisite for pivoting to 2e, 2f, or 2g. Every non-dual-degree option on the table requires a conferred Hobart BA:

- **2e (Syracuse ECS 4+2)** — direct-admission pathway explicitly requires a completed Hobart BA + 3.2 GPA + faculty recommendation + bridge course.
- **2f (Cal State BSME)** — the BA itself requires 32 courses, and without a partner school feeding courses back, Jack must self-complete at Hobart. Separately, CSU ME lower-div prep (Statics / Dynamics / Circuits / Mech of Materials) differs from the physics BA; the 6-year cost model in [`README.md`](./README.md) assumes Jack arrives at CSU with the BA conferred and absorbs the ME lower-div gap at upper-division standing.
- **2g (Hobart BA → OCS → MS)** — OCS (Navy, Marine, Army, NUPOC) **requires a conferred bachelor's degree at commissioning**. A Y3 physics-BA-in-progress does not qualify.

The 2f-vs-2g decision then happens **during** Y4, not at the Y3 fork — both application cycles run during senior year, and they're not mutually exclusive until commit time in spring of Y4.

### ⚠ Anti-pattern: skipping Y4 Hobart to start Cal State early

A tempting simplification after a failed junior-year fork: "if Columbia 2a rejects at Y3, skip Y4 Hobart and start Cal State a year earlier to save time and money." **This does not work** — the cost-benefit is decisively negative:

- **Gain:** Y4 Hobart (~$46K all-in) replaced by a CSU year (~$29K all-in). Net savings ~$17K.
- **Lose:**
  - The Hobart BA credential entirely (Jack exits with a partial transcript, no bachelor's).
  - The 4th and final IRA rowing season (his last eligible year under the 4-in-5 clock).
  - The Y4 $41K Hobart merit scholarship.
  - Eligibility for 2g entirely (no BA = no OCS commissioning).

Y4 Hobart is cheap insurance: a light academic year (physics major largely done in Y3, remaining ~8 courses are goal slots + free electives — see [`hobart-plan.md`](./hobart-plan.md#hws-y4-senior-year-finish-the-physics-ba) for the Y4 term plan) that converts a failed dual-degree attempt into a completed BA plus a viable 2f *or* 2g pivot.

## The eligibility reframing that changes the pitch

With the IRA 4-season cap and Ivy League conference policy (no redshirts, no grad-student varsity) properly modeled, **both Ivy paths (2a Columbia, 2b Dartmouth) deliver the same 4 total competitive seasons** — and only 1 of those 4 is at the Ivy partner. The binding constraint is a 4-in-5 eligibility clock that counts prior intercollegiate competition. The differences between the two Ivy paths are:

- **Peak Ivy season:** 1 year of Columbia LW (Y4) vs. 1 year of Dartmouth LW (Y3).
- **Credential earned:** Columbia SEAS BS vs. Dartmouth Thayer BE (both elite, both ABET).
- **Cost:** ~$304K (2a) vs. ~$316K (2b) — 2b is **slightly more expensive** because both Y3 and Y5 at Dartmouth are billed at full Dartmouth tuition; the prior model assumed Y3 routed through Hobart's scholarship-discounted tuition as a Hobart-registered exchange year, but that scholarship-transfer assumption is now rejected. The 2b case no longer rests on cost — see [`README.md`](./README.md#cost-assumptions-used-in-the-table) and the comparison-table 2b row.
- **Admit risk:** Columbia 3.50 / Dartmouth ~3.30 GPA gate; both are competitive transfer reviews. Dartmouth's sophomore-year deadline (Feb 1 Y2) hits ~10 months earlier than Columbia's junior-year deadline (Feb 1 Y3), enabling the portfolio strategy.

**Syracuse 2e adds a third primary path** without adding any rowing season at Syracuse (heavyweight-only IRA + grad eligibility-exhausted under the 4-in-5 clock = same outcome as 2f/2g). The 2e pitch is therefore: **same 4 IRA seasons at Hobart, near-automatic graduate-engineering admit at 3.2 GPA, 2 years of MS credential at Syracuse ECS (~#70).** Lower credential ceiling than the Ivy paths, but materially lower admit risk.
