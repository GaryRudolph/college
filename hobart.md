# If Hobart: the junior-year fork

*Follow-on to [`README.md`](./README.md). Read this only if the starting-school choice is Hobart — otherwise the content here doesn't apply. For the full research base, see [`background.md`](./background.md).*

## The sub-decision

If the starting-school answer is Hobart, the next question is which dual-degree partner(s) to target at the junior-year fork (sophomore year for Dartmouth). The portfolio strategy:

> **Apply to Dartmouth sophomore year, then Columbia + WashU junior year, with Cal State BSME as the end-state safety net.**

This gives Jack two distinct Ivy shots plus a near-automatic fallback, without requiring any single application to succeed.

---

## The three dual-degree options that also clear the rowing bar

- **Option 2a — Hobart → Columbia 3:2 (~$322K all-in, 5 yrs, 4 rowing seasons, 1 at Columbia LW).** Elite Ivy engineering credential (SEAS BS). 3 Hobart IRA seasons + 1 Columbia LW season (Y4 only; Y5 Ivy-ineligible as grad). 3.50 GPA gate at junior-year fork. Cleaner rowing arc than Dartmouth — no Y4 interruption.
- **Option 2b — Hobart → Dartmouth 2:1:1:1 (~$334K all-in, 5 yrs, 3–4 rowing seasons, 1 at Dartmouth LW).** Elite Ivy engineering credential (Thayer BE). 2 Hobart seasons + 1 Dartmouth LW (Y3) + optional 1 Hobart openweight (Y4, burns his 4th season) + no Y5 rowing (Ivy-ineligible). Highest total cost of the Hobart paths. ~3.30 GPA gate at the **sophomore-year** fork — roughly 10 months earlier than the Columbia decision, which enables the portfolio strategy (try Dartmouth first, pivot junior year if rejected). Y4 rowing is Hobart openweight only, and Dartmouth Y3 grades appear on the transcript for all downstream fallback applications.
- **Option 2c — Hobart → WashU 3:3 (~$320K all-in, 6 yrs, 4 rowing seasons, 1 at nationally competitive ACRA).** Strong-but-not-Ivy engineering credential, three degrees (BA + BS + MS), near-automatic admit at 3.25 GPA. 3 Hobart IRA seasons + 1 WashU ACRA season (Y4 only; Y5–Y6 as grad are eligibility-exhausted under ACRA's 4-in-5 clock). WashU's men raced to an ACRA M4+ national championship in 2024 and M2- bronze in 2025 — a step up from WPI's DIII rowing, a step below Ivy LW. **Lowest-risk of the three** (admit is near-automatic) and roughly the same cost as 2a despite adding an MS.

## The other Hobart forks

- **Option 2d — Hobart → RPI 3:2 (~$322K all-in, 5 yrs).** Engineering-only play. RPI crew is recreational with no ACRA/IRA footprint — rowing drops off after Hobart Y3. Near-automatic at ~3.00 GPA. **Dominated by WashU (2c)** on essentially every axis: 2c is roughly the same cost, adds a master's degree, and keeps rowing nationally competitive for the post-Hobart year. Keep 2d as an alternative if WashU doesn't fit for other reasons, but it's not the default.
- **Option 2e — Hobart BA → Cal State BSME (~$266K all-in, 6 yrs).** The end-state safety net if every dual-degree fork fails. 4 years Hobart IRA rowing + Cal State BSME at CA resident tuition. Cheapest of the Hobart paths. Rowing ends at Hobart; Cal State crew is club-level. Low-moderate admit risk (CSU typically needs 2.7 GPA + prereqs). Also a natural entry point for NUPOC or unrestricted-line OCS — see [`military.md`](./military.md) for entry-path detail. *Note: if Jack commutes from home during the 2 Cal State years, this path drops by ~$40K (saves on R&B) to ~$226K.*
- **Option 2f — Hobart BA → OCS → post-service MS (~$208K undergrad + $0 grad, 4 yrs college then service).** Engineering reputation: **Solid to Elite** — Hobart BA alone is solid; the path unlocks a tuition-free MS in engineering at Dartmouth Thayer, Cornell, or Stanford after service, which lands the final credential in the elite tier. Rowing: same as 2e — 4 Hobart IRA seasons, ends at graduation. Cost: 4 years of Hobart at ~$52K/yr (tuition + R&B) ≈ **~$208K out-of-pocket college cost**, then commissioned service pays the rest. See the three mechanisms below.

### How Option 2f pays for the engineering credential

Three mechanisms stack to make a post-service MS effectively free at the right schools:

1. **Post-9/11 GI Bill (after 3 years of active duty, 100% benefit).** Pays **$29,920.95/year for private schools** (2025–26 rate; adjusts annually) and **100% of in-state tuition at public schools**. Plus a monthly housing allowance (~BAH E-5 with dependents for the school's ZIP code) and $1,000/year for books. Covers up to 36 months of full-time enrollment. **Transferable to a spouse or dependents after 6 years of service with a 4-year additional commitment.**
2. **Yellow Ribbon Program** (private-school top-up to the GI Bill).
  - **Dartmouth:** uncapped — covers full remaining tuition, unlimited participants (A+ rating).
  - **Cornell:** covers the remainder, capped at ~100 veterans on campus, first-come-first-served for grad students.
  - **Columbia:** ~$8,000/year additional. Most veterans enter via the General Studies program; grad school (SEAS, etc.) participation varies.
  - **Stanford** (for reference): $16K–$20K additional for graduate engineering, uncapped.
  - **Practical implication:** **a veteran can attend Dartmouth Thayer, Cornell Engineering, or Stanford Engineering for near-zero tuition** if they have 100% GI Bill eligibility.
3. **Navy-funded graduate education (active duty).** While still in service, officers can apply for:
  - **CIVINS / Fleet Scholars Education Program (FSEP):** Navy pays full tuition + salary for up to 24 months at any accredited U.S. grad school (MIT, Stanford, Purdue, etc.) for a master's. Competitive, typically O-3 / O-4 officers with strong records.
  - **Naval Postgraduate School (NPS):** Monterey, CA. Navy-internal grad school offering master's in engineering fields, tuition-free for selected officers. Another standard path.
  - **Tuition Assistance (TA):** $4,500/year cap — useful for part-time coursework, not a full degree funder.

**Net cost implication for Option 2f:**

- 4 years of Hobart BA (tuition + R&B): ~$208K.
- Military service: paid (officer salary during service).
- Post-service MS: **$0 tuition** at Dartmouth Thayer via GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon (the most generous Ivy for veterans), or near-zero at Cornell / Stanford. Plus housing allowance and books stipend during school.
- Or active-duty master's: $0 tuition at Stanford / MIT / Purdue via CIVINS / FSEP, while being paid.
- **Total out-of-pocket all-in cost for a Hobart BA + elite engineering MS: ~$208K.** Total tuition value of the GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon benefits: **$120K–$300K+ depending on school and path**, before counting salary.

**What makes 2f distinct from 2a–2e:** the engineering credential is delayed by service time (typically 3–5 years) but ends up elite without needing the 3.50 GPA transfer gate (2a), sophomore-year Dartmouth review (2b), or 6 years of continuous college (2c, 2e). The trade is the military service commitment itself, not the credential quality.

**When 2f is the right play:** if Jack is already drawn to military service (especially nuclear / submarine via NUPOC, or Navy officer more broadly), and wants an elite engineering credential without an undergraduate transfer gamble. Also works as a **post-hoc pivot from 2e** — if every dual-degree fork fails and he lands at the Hobart BA, post-grad OCS + GI Bill Yellow Ribbon is materially better than a Cal State BSME for the final credential.

## The eligibility reframing that changes the pitch

With the IRA 4-season cap, Ivy League conference policy (no redshirts, no grad-student varsity), and ACRA's own 4-in-5 clock all properly modeled, **all three rowing-fit options (2a, 2b, 2c) deliver the same 4 total competitive seasons.** The binding constraint in every case is a 4-in-5 eligibility clock that counts prior intercollegiate competition. The differences between the three paths are:

- **Peak level of the final season:** Ivy LW (2a, 2b) vs. nationally competitive ACRA club (2c).
- **Credential earned:** Ivy BS/BE (2a, 2b) vs. WashU BS + MS (2c).
- **Cost:** Ivy paths ~$322K (2a) / ~$334K (2b) vs. WashU ~$320K (2c) — roughly comparable once you factor in the Ivy's single extra year vs. WashU's extra two. WashU's per-year cost is lower, so the MS comes "for free" in cost terms.
- **Admit risk:** Ivy 3.50 / ~3.30 GPA gate at a competitive transfer review vs. WashU ~3.25 GPA near-automatic.

The previous framing of WashU as "3 extra years of rowing after Hobart" was wrong — ACRA's clock prevents it. So the WashU path's real pitch is **cheaper + lower-risk + adds a master's degree**, not "more rowing."
