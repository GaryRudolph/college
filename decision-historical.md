## The decision backdrop

Jack wanted three things simultaneously:

1. **Row at a D1-like level.** Being in a B or C boat at a serious program is fine. Being in the top boat at a recreational club is not. The target is real rowing culture — dedicated training, serious competition, coaching depth.
2. **Optionality for an Ivy-degree education with rowing stacked on top.** Not a hard requirement, but a real want if reachable: spend at least some of the college years at an Ivy, race lightweight at one of the Ivy LW programs, come out the other side with an Ivy credential.
3. **End up with an engineering degree** — ABET-accredited BS preferred, and the credential must be real enough to support a standard engineering job market entry (internships, new-grad hiring, PE licensure if that becomes relevant).

Those three goals pull in different directions. Most paths that maximize one compromise another:

- **WPI** maximizes engineering certainty and is the cheapest path, but its rowing peaks at upper-tier DIII (IRCA D3 top-10) — not the D1-level target, and no Ivy upside on top.
- **UBC** maximizes engineering + lifestyle, but realistic LW walk-on was uncertain (~4 slots) — the base case collapsed to "engineering + lifestyle, no rowing," which traded away priority #1.
- **Hobart** was the only starting school that kept all three priorities live: IRA-system D1/D2 racing at the base, a 1-year Ivy LW shot via Columbia 3:2 (Option 2a) or Dartmouth 2:1:1:1 (Option 2b), and an engineering credential at the end (BS via 2a/2b, BSME via 2f Cal State, MS via 2e Syracuse, or post-service MS via 2g OCS).

The decision converged on Hobart because it was the only option that didn't force an early trade between rowing ambition, Ivy optionality, and the engineering credential. Every other option closed one of the three doors on day one. Hobart's cost (~$242K–$316K depending on fork) was a premium over WPI (~$220K), but the premium bought two live priorities that WPI didn't reach.

## The 3-way starting-school framework

The real decision was **where Jack showed up as a freshman.** Everything else — dual-degree transfer, fallback, military entry — was a sub-branch off that first choice.

1. **WPI** — Strong engineering, low risk, mid-competitive DIII rowing. Simple: focus on education and rowing.
   - **Cost:** ~$220K all-in (cheapest path)
   - **Years:** 4
   - **Curriculum:** Engineering from day one; project-based, minimal writing load. Best fit for Jack's stated preference against writing-heavy coursework.
   - **Trade:** rowing peaks at mid-high pack DIII
2. **Hobart** — Solid IRA-system rowing with a path to **both** elite rowing (only 1 year at Columbia or Dartmouth LW) **and/or** solid-to-elite engineering (Columbia 3:2, Dartmouth 2:1:1:1, Syracuse ECS 4+2 MS, or Cal State BSME). Multiple fallbacks at the junior-year fork — see [`hobart.md`](./hobart.md) for the per-option detail.
   - **Cost:** ~$242K–$316K depending on fork (with $41K/yr merit scholarship applied for years Jack is enrolled at Hobart — Option 2b's Y3 at Dartmouth is billed at full Dartmouth tuition, scholarship does not apply)
   - **Years:** 5–6 depending on fork
   - **Curriculum:** Physics-major-first at Hobart Y1–Y3 (~60–75% STEM by course count; the 2:1:1:1 Dartmouth fork is STEM-heavier than the 3:2 Columbia fork). Only 1 mandatory writing-intensive course (the First-Year Seminar); the rest of the breadth is flexible topic-choice across 6 "aspirational goals." Less of a mismatch with Jack's writing preference than a prescribed-core liberal-arts school would be.
   - **Trade:** GPA gate sophomore/junior year; worst case (every dual-degree fork fails) is Hobart BA + Cal State BSME at ~$242K / 6 yrs — still a viable landing spot
3. **UBC** — Solid engineering (QS #25 globally, #2 in Canada), elite Canadian varsity rowing **if** he makes the team, and an amazing lifestyle / location.
   - **Cost:** ~$268K (USD)
   - **Years:** 4
   - **Curriculum:** Engineering from day one (BASc); limited humanities / writing requirements. Similar fit to WPI on curriculum style.
   - **Trade:** Jack's own assessment was that walking on was uncertain (LW roster ~4 slots). Realistic base case: no rowing at UBC. Treat as "engineering + lifestyle, rowing optional."

**A military option could attach to any of the three paths** — applying to USNA in summer 2026 as a wildcard, or post-grad OCS/NUPOC after any of them. It didn't compete with the starting-school choice, so the 3-way framing held. See [`military.md`](./military.md) for role types, entry paths, and the GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon cost math (Option 2g is the specific Hobart + OCS instance).

### Which fits which priority

| Priority weighted highest                                                | Starting school |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------- |
| Engineering certainty, cost, simplicity, done in 4 years                 | **WPI**         |
| Rowing ambition (esp. Ivy LW) with engineering upside, done in 5-6 years | **Hobart**      |
| Engineering-first with lifestyle and *possible* elite row                | **UBC**         |

Jack's priority weighting landed on **Rowing ambition with engineering upside** — hence Hobart.

## Why not WPI (pre-decision rationale)

WPI's rowing stepped up materially in the 2026 season (IRCA D3 #10 V8 Wk 4 Apr 29; won McCarthy/McGee Cup Apr 25 over #11 Coast Guard + Boston College — first signature 2026 V8 win, racing close to ranked Williams / Tufts / Marietta earlier in the season under HC Larry Noble in his 25th season — full detail in [`hobart-vs-wpi-rowing.md`](./hobart-vs-wpi-rowing.md)) and genuinely satisfied *competitive rowing program with real rowing culture*.

But it did so at the **D3 tier** — Hobart races a tier above (IRCA D1/D2 universe; 3V8 #22 + 2V8 #25 D1/D2 Wk 4, V8 currently unranked but races D1 fields — Dartmouth, BU, Temple, Holy Cross, Marist; under HC Chris Kerber, 4× IRA national champion at Cornell LW, in Year 3 of a deliberate program elevation), and only the Hobart paths put a 1-year Ivy LW shot on top of that base.

The Hobart premium over WPI was **~$22K (vs. the Hobart→Cal State floor) / ~$84K (vs. 2a Columbia) / ~$96K (vs. 2b Dartmouth)**. The case for paying that premium held for Jack's priority weighting (D1-level rowing + Ivy LW optionality). WPI would have become the right answer if Jack had reweighted toward engineering-certainty + cost-floor + 25-year-HC-stability over rowing-tier ceiling and Ivy LW upside — he didn't.

## Why not UBC (pre-decision rationale)

Jack's own assessment was that LW walk-on was uncertain (~4 slots), so the realistic base case was "engineering + lifestyle, no rowing." That collapsed one of his two stated priorities, which was a worse outcome on his own terms than Hobart's GPA-gate risk. UBC remained attractive on engineering + lifestyle axes, but without rowing it wasn't actually reaching his priority weighting.

## Military option (pre-decision framing)

Two military options could attach to any of the primary paths rather than replacing the starting-school choice:

- **USNA this summer (wildcard that preempted everything above).** If Jack secured a Congressional nomination and got admitted, USNA would change the game — **tuition, room & board, and medical all covered, plus a monthly stipend** (effectively $0 out-of-pocket), ABET engineering BS, DI lightweight rowing at Eastern Sprints, 5-year service commitment. **Important disclaimer: USNA does not accept transfer credit.** If admitted after his Hobart freshman year, Jack would **restart as a plebe** and graduate Class of 2031 instead of 2030. Admit rate ~8% plus the nomination hurdle, so low-probability, but if it lands it preempts the 3-way decision. Worth applying in parallel; rejection didn't close any door.
- **OCS after graduation (if military service became an aspiration).** Any bachelor's degree qualifies for unrestricted-line OCS (SWO, aviation, NFO, intel, supply). An ABET engineering BS additionally qualifies for engineering-designator OCS (EDO, CEC). This route is available after any primary path — no decision needed at matriculation, no action during college.

## Curriculum-fit framing (pre-decision, all 3 schools)

Jack stated a preference against writing-heavy coursework. Engineering curricula are STEM-dense and don't vary much across programs; the dimension that *does* vary is the **non-STEM load** — especially at Hobart, where it also differs meaningfully between the 2a Columbia and 2b Dartmouth forks.

### WPI (Option 1) — Engineering from day 1

- 4-year ABET BS structured around the **WPI Plan**: 7-week terms, each culminating in a project deliverable rather than a final exam.
- Humanities/Arts requirement: **6 courses total**, typically a themed 5-course sequence + 1 capstone. Topic-flexible; not uniformly writing-intensive.
- **~80% of the degree is STEM.** Closest fit to Jack's stated curriculum preference.

### Hobart (Options 2a–2g) — flexible breadth-goal system, not a prescribed core

Per the [HWS catalogue](https://www.hws.edu/catalogue/curriculum.aspx):

- **32 courses total** — 4/semester × 8 semesters.
- **First-Year Seminar (FYS)** — 1 writing-intensive course, mandatory, pass with C−. This is the only strictly writing-heavy requirement.
- **Physics BA major — 12 courses** (5 required physics: PHYS 150, 160, 270, 285, 383 + Calc I & II + 5 electives including capstone). Jack's AP scores (Calc AB, BC, CS A + Physics C Mech/E&M assumed 5/5 on May 2026 exams) should wipe out 4–5 of those, leaving ~8 physics-major courses at Hobart.
- **8 Educational Goals** — 2 integrated (critical thinking, communication) taught across the curriculum + 6 aspirational (quantitative reasoning, scientific inquiry, artistic process, social inequalities, cultural difference, ethical judgment). The physics major covers the first 2 automatically. The remaining 4 require **≥5 distinct courses total**, with broad course-choice latitude — there is no Columbia-Core-style prescribed sequence.

**Per-semester, either Hobart path is ~1 non-STEM course per term** (~25% of that term's load). The FYS is the only strictly writing-intensive requirement; other goal courses can be chosen as reading-heavy rather than writing-heavy. Full Hobart-fork-specific detail in [`README.md`](./README.md#curriculum-fit).

### UBC (Option 3) — Engineering from day 1

- 4-year BASc: common 1st-year engineering foundation, declared discipline in Y2. Limited humanities / complementary studies requirement (~3–5 non-engineering courses total across the 4 years).
- **~85% of the degree is STEM.** Closest to WPI on curriculum style.
