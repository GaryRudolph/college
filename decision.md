## What we're deciding

Jack wants to be in a **competitive rowing program with a real rowing culture** — being in a B or C boat at a serious program is fine — **and** he wants a Bachelor of Engineering. Those two goals pull in different directions, and most of his viable paths stack conditional hurdles — 3:2 transfer admits, GPA thresholds, rowing eligibility — on top of each other. The finalists are WPI (the "just done" anchor), Hobart (four flavors — Columbia, Dartmouth, WashU, or RPI via dual-degree, plus a state-school BSME fallback), and UBC. A military option is covered in a separate section after the comparison, since it can attach to any of these paths rather than competing with them.

### Critical eligibility note: why the 5- and 6-year paths do NOT mean 5–6 years of racing

Three separate eligibility systems apply across Jack's option set, and **all three cap competition at 4 seasons completed within 5 calendar years of first full-time collegiate enrollment:**

1. **IRA** (governs Hobart, Columbia, Dartmouth men's LW). Adopts IRA rules as baseline: 4 seasons in 5 years, freshman participation counts as a season used.
2. **Ivy League conference overlay** (Columbia, Dartmouth — NOT Hobart):
  - **Graduate students are ineligible for varsity athletics.** A one-time COVID waiver in 2021–22 was explicitly not renewed. Columbia's 2025–26 LW roster confirms this — no grad students.
  - **No redshirts.** If enrolled during the season, it counts whether he races or not.
3. **ACRA** (governs WashU, club rowing). Its own written 4-in-5 clock: "four years of eligibility, which must be completed during the first 10 semesters or 15 quarters after the student is enrolled in a collegiate institution." Prior IRA/NCAA seasons at another school **count against the ACRA clock** — ACRA Rule #2 says "competing for an institution in any one event within the academic year is enough to constitute one year of eligibility." ACRA does allow grad students to race *if* they have eligibility remaining and are within the 5-year window (Rule #5), but you can only row for 4 years.

**Practical implication for each path:**

- **Columbia 3:2 — 1 year of Columbia LW racing (Y4).**  
Hobart Y1–3 burns 3 of 4 IRA seasons. Y4 at Columbia is senior undergrad — eligible. Y5 at Columbia is graduate standing — **Ivy-ineligible**, and Ivies don't allow redshirting Y4 to preserve for Y5.
- **Dartmouth 2:1:1:1 — 1 year of Dartmouth LW racing (Y3).**  
Hobart Y1–2 burns 2 seasons. Y3 at Dartmouth as junior undergrad — eligible. Y4 back at Hobart — eligible, but openweight only. Y5 at Dartmouth is graduate standing — **Ivy-ineligible**.
- **WashU 3:3 — 1 year of WashU ACRA racing (Y4)**  
Hobart Y1–3 burns 3 of 4 ACRA seasons. Y4 at WashU uses his 4th and final ACRA season AND is clock year 4 of 5. Y5 at WashU (grad year 1): 0 seasons remaining under ACRA Rule #2, and clock year 5 is the last. Y6 is outside the 5-year clock entirely.

## Military option

The military option is deliberately separate from the comparison above because **it can attach to any of the five primary paths** rather than competing with them. But before evaluating entry paths, there's a prior question: **what does Jack want out of military service?** The answer shapes which entry path to pursue and which service branch makes sense. It also determines whether the service pays for a BS/MS in engineering afterward — which for several role types, it does.

### Step 2: Can military service pay for a BS or MS in engineering afterward? Yes — three mechanisms.

This meaningfully changes the cost math for Option 2e (Hobart BA → State School BSME) and for anyone who enters without an engineering BS.

1. **Post-9/11 GI Bill (after 3 years of active duty, 100% benefit).** Pays **$29,920.95/year for private schools** (2025–26 rate; adjusts annually) and **100% of in-state tuition at public schools**. Plus a monthly housing allowance (~BAH E-5 with dependents for the school's ZIP code) and $1,000/year for books. Covers up to 36 months of full-time enrollment. **Transferable to a spouse or dependents after 6 years of service with a 4-year additional commitment.**
2. **Yellow Ribbon Program** (private-school top-up to the GI Bill).
  - **Dartmouth:** uncapped — covers full remaining tuition, unlimited participants (A+ rating).
    - **Cornell:** covers the remainder, capped at ~100 veterans on campus, first-come-first-served for grad students.
    - **Columbia:** ~$8,000/year additional. Most veterans enter via the General Studies program; grad school (SEAS, etc.) participation varies.
    - **Stanford** (for reference): $16K–$20K additional for graduate engineering, uncapped.
    - **Practical implication:** **a veteran can attend Dartmouth Thayer, Cornell Engineering, or Stanford Engineering for near-zero tuition** if they have 100% GI Bill eligibility.
3. **Navy-funded graduate education (active duty).** While still in service, officers can apply for:
  - **CIVINS / Fleet Scholars Education Program (FSEP):** Navy pays full tuition + salary for up to 24 months at any accredited U.S. grad school (MIT, Stanford, Purdue, etc.) for a master's. Competitive, typically O-3 / O-4 officers with strong records.
    - **Naval Postgraduate School (NPS):** Monterey, CA. Navy-internal grad school offering master's in engineering fields, tuition-free for selected officers. Another standard path.
    - **Tuition Assistance (TA):** $4,500/year cap — useful for part-time coursework, not a full degree funder.

**Net cost implication for the military pathway:**

- If Jack enters as an officer and serves 3+ years, he can effectively get a **BS or MS at a top engineering school for $0 tuition** via GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon at Dartmouth (the most generous Ivy).
- If he makes O-3 / O-4 and gets selected for CIVINS, **a fully-funded master's at Stanford / MIT / Purdue while being paid** is a real possibility.
- Total tuition value of these benefits: **$120K–$300K+ depending on school and path**, before counting salary.

### Step 3: Entry paths (how he gets in)

#### (a) USNA this summer (if Jack can secure a nomination)

- **Reality check:** USNA does not accept transfer credit. If admitted, Jack **restarts as a plebe** — his Hobart freshman year is essentially lost academically (though not developmentally). He'd be Class of 2031 instead of Class of 2030.
- **Admit rate:** ~8%, plus the nomination hurdle from a senator, representative, or the VP.
- **Timing:** Application window typically opens April with a January 31 deadline; nominations require parallel tracks started in the spring.
- **Engineering:** USNA is an ABET-accredited engineering school; ~60% of midshipmen major in STEM. **Gets him a BS in engineering with no tuition cost and a 5-year active-duty commitment.**
- **Rowing:** Navy has a strong heavyweight program. Navy men's lightweight competes at Eastern Sprints; smaller roster than Harvard/Columbia/Dartmouth but legitimate DI lightweight competition.
- **Role implication:** USNA funnels into most designators (SWO, aviation, submarine, USMC, NUPOC-equivalent). Good if Jack is open across role types.
- **Risk:** Very high on the admit gate, very low on the "will he get a BS in engineering" question once admitted. The 5-year service commitment is a real life choice, not just a degree plan.

#### (b) NUPOC — junior or senior year of college

- **Eligibility:** calc-based physics, calc through DiffEq, ~3.0+ GPA (higher for NR Engineer at ~3.5+). **Physics majors are a standard NUPOC profile** — Hobart Applied Physics qualifies.
- **Benefits while in school:** $5,000+/month stipend, up to $38K signing bonus, total up to ~$168K before graduation.
- **Service:** 5 years active + 3 years inactive reserve.
- **Role:** Submarine officer, SWO-Nuclear, or Naval Reactors Engineer.
- **Best fit:** If Jack is drawn to the technical / nuclear role.

#### (c) OCS after graduation — any bachelor's degree

- **Unrestricted line (SWO, aviation, NFO, intel, supply):** requires a bachelor's in any field. Physics BA qualifies. Jack would commission as a line officer, 4-year commitment.
- **Engineering designator (EDO, CEC, Meteorology/Oceanography):** requires ABET-EAC engineering bachelor's. **Physics BA does NOT qualify.** WPI, UBC, Columbia/Dartmouth/WashU/RPI engineering BS all qualify.
- **Best fit:** If Jack wants operational leadership (SWO, aviation) or real engineering practice (CEC, EDO) — and has the right degree.

#### (d) Army Direct Commissioning — Engineer Officer (post-grad)

- **Requires:** ABET engineering BS + 3 years of relevant work experience. Longer term option — not a path at age 22, but a path by ~25–26.
- **Useful to know exists** — it's a second-chance commission track if Navy OCS doesn't work out.

#### ROTC — explicitly excluded

ROTC (NROTC / AROTC / AFROTC) was considered and **ruled out for capacity reasons.** Engineering coursework plus serious rowing is already a full academic + athletic load; layering ROTC's 0400 PT, weekly drill, summer training, and service obligations on top would compromise both GPA and rowing performance. The right military entry paths for Jack are **post-grad OCS/NUPOC** (applied for junior or senior year) or **USNA** (freshman-restart). ROTC is not on the table.

### Practical recommendation on the military option

- **First: get clarity from Jack on what role appeals to him.** Is it nuclear/technical (→ NUPOC), operational leadership (→ SWO/aviation), engineering in uniform (→ CEC/EDO), or SpecOps/combat (→ USMC/SEAL)? This should be a conversation, not a research task.
- **Pursue USNA application this spring/summer in parallel** with whatever primary choice is made. It costs ~$0 beyond nomination legwork and the application effort. Rejection doesn't close any door; acceptance is a meaningful fork Jack can evaluate with actual admission in hand.
- **NUPOC doesn't require any action now** — it's a post-graduation option (applications typically submitted junior/senior year), and the eligibility windows extend up to 42 months past bachelor's completion. It just needs to stay on the radar as the end-state safety net for Option 2e.
- **Treat post-service GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon as a real part of the cost math.** A common pattern: BS somewhere affordable → commission → serve 3–5 years → **use GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon for a Dartmouth Thayer / Cornell / Stanford MS in engineering at near-zero tuition**. This is especially relevant for Options 1, 4, and 5, where the undergrad credential is less prestigious (or less expensive) than an Ivy BS and can be topped with a near-free elite MS.
