### Critical eligibility note: why "5 years at the Ivy" does NOT mean 2 years of Ivy rowing

Men's lightweight rowing at Hobart, Columbia, and Dartmouth is governed by **IRA** (Intercollegiate Rowing Association), not the NCAA directly. IRA has its own eligibility bylaws, which adopt NCAA rules as the baseline unless amended. On top of IRA, the **Ivy League** layers its own conference-specific policies that apply at Columbia and Dartmouth (but not Hobart).

The rule stack that applies to Jack:

1. **IRA baseline: 4 seasons of competition** (adopted from the NCAA manual via the IRA bylaws). Participation at the freshman/novice level still counts as a season used. Clock runs for 5 calendar years from first full-time enrollment.
2. **Ivy League conference overlay** (Columbia, Dartmouth — not Hobart):
    - **Graduate students are ineligible for varsity athletics.** The Ivy League restricts athletic opportunities to undergraduates. A one-time COVID waiver in 2021–22 let that year's seniors race as grad students, and was explicitly not renewed. Columbia's 2025–26 lightweight roster confirms current enforcement — no GS designations on the men's LW roster.
    - **No redshirts.** If enrolled and medically able during the season, that season counts as used whether he races or not.

**Practical implication for each path:**

- **Columbia 3:2:** Hobart Y1–3 burns 3 of 4 IRA seasons. Y4 at Columbia is senior undergrad — eligible. Y5 at Columbia is graduate standing — **Ivy-ineligible**, and Ivies don't allow redshirting Y4 to preserve for Y5. **Result: 1 year of Columbia LW racing (Y4).**
- **Dartmouth 2:1:1:1:** Hobart Y1–2 burns 2 seasons. Y3 at Dartmouth as junior undergrad — eligible. Y4 back at Hobart — eligible, but openweight only. Y5 at Dartmouth is graduate standing — **Ivy-ineligible**. **Result: 1 year of Dartmouth LW racing (Y3).**

The IRA 4-season cap alone would still allow Y5 rowing at the Ivy in theory. The hard stop is the **Ivy League conference policy barring graduate athletes**, which trumps IRA eligibility. At a non-Ivy IRA school (Hobart, Harvard Extension, etc.), grad-student rowing is possible — at Columbia and Dartmouth, it is not.

This does not kill either Ivy path — 1 year at an elite Ivy LW program is still a meaningful rowing experience — but it weakens the "elite rowing throughout" framing. The WashU fallback (Option 2a) operates outside IRA entirely (ACRA club rules) and delivers **3 additional years of competitive rowing after Hobart**, more total years of competitive rowing than either Ivy path.

## The decision framework

### First question: which options clear the rowing bar?

Jack's criterion is "competitive rowing program with real rowing culture; B/C boat is fine." Filtering the option set, with the added constraint that **Jack is a lightweight** — so programs without a meaningful LW roster don't actually deliver the "B/C boat at a serious program" experience for him:

- **Clears the bar, with 1 year of Ivy LW racing:** Option 1a (Hobart → Columbia — 3 years Hobart IRA, then 1 year Columbia LW Y4; Y5 Ivy-ineligible as grad) and Option 1b (Hobart → Dartmouth — 2 years Hobart, 1 year Dartmouth LW Y3, 1 year Hobart openweight Y4; Y5 Ivy-ineligible as grad).
- **Clears the bar, sustained:** Option 2a (Hobart → WashU — IRA-system Hobart, then nationally competitive ACRA club).
- **Clears the bar for most of the program, drops off at the end:** Option 2b (Hobart → RPI — 3 years of Hobart, then rec club), Option 3 (Hobart only → state school — 3–4 years of Hobart, then club).
- **Solid but a tier below Hobart:** Option 4 (WPI — real DIII program, just not elite).
- **Does not clear the bar for Jack specifically:** Option 6 (UBC — elite program in the abstract, but **Jack is not confident he can walk on at all**, and even if he did, only 4 LW slots makes lightweight racing unlikely; realistic base case is no rowing).

**The paths that fully match Jack's stated rowing ambition are Options 1 and 2a.**

### Second question: how much engineering-reputation premium are you willing to pay for a path that clears the rowing bar?

Between the two that fully clear the bar:

- **~$242K, 5 years, Ivy BS, 1 year of Ivy LW racing (Y4):** Option 1a (Hobart → Columbia). Elite engineering credential, 3.50 GPA gate at junior-year fork.
- **~$242K, 5 years, Ivy BE, 1 year of Ivy LW racing (Y3):** Option 1b (Hobart → Dartmouth). Elite engineering credential, ~3.30 GPA gate at sophomore-year fork; strategic value in the earlier decision.

Strong-but-not-Ivy credential, near-automatic admit, extra master's degree, sustained competitive rowing (IRA-system → national ACRA club). **Cheaper, lower-risk, AND delivers more total years of competitive rowing than either Ivy path.**

UBC remains a viable **engineering-first** fallback if the rowing priority is willing to be relaxed, but it should not be positioned as a path that meets Jack's rowing ambition.

## Military option

The military option is deliberately separate from the comparison above because **it can attach to any of the five primary paths** rather than competing with them. But before evaluating entry paths, there's a prior question: **what does Jack want out of military service?** The answer shapes which entry path to pursue and which service branch makes sense. It also determines whether the service pays for a BS/MS in engineering afterward — which for several role types, it does.

### Step 1: What kind of role does Jack want?

Military officer roles sort roughly into four buckets. The best role depends on what Jack finds compelling, not just what's available.

| Role type | What the day-to-day looks like | Best entry | Engineering connection |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Technical / nuclear** — Submarine officer, SWO-Nuclear, Naval Reactors Engineer | Running a nuclear propulsion plant or reactor engineering work. Long sea tours for sub/SWO-N; HQ engineering work for NR. Demanding technical standards, small peer group, prestige track. | **NUPOC** (junior/senior year) or **USNA** | Most technical path. Nuclear training is effectively a graduate-level engineering course. |
| **Operational line** — Surface Warfare Officer, Naval Aviator / NFO, Marine Corps | Leading sailors or Marines, standing watch, flying aircraft, operational planning. More leadership-and-people than pure engineering. | **USNA** or **OCS** (post-grad) | Engineering degree helpful but not required. |
| **Engineering duty** — Engineering Duty Officer (EDO), Civil Engineer Corps (CEC), Army Engineer Officer | Ship design / acquisition / maintenance (EDO); public works, construction, NAVFAC projects (CEC); combat engineering, construction, infrastructure (Army). Closest to civilian engineering practice inside the service. | **OCS** (EDO via SWO→EDO pipeline; CEC directly with ABET BS), **Army Direct Commissioning** for engineers | **Requires ABET engineering BS** for the engineering-specific designators. Physics BA does not qualify. |
| **Special operations / combat arms** | SEAL, EOD, infantry, combat engineer. Physical-first, high-commitment culture. | USNA or OCS | Engineering not central. |

**Key question for Jack:** is the appeal **technical prestige and nuclear engineering** (→ NUPOC / submarine / NR), **operational leadership and ships-at-sea** (→ SWO or USMC), **doing real engineering in uniform** (→ CEC or EDO), or **the combat-arms / SpecOps track** (→ USMC, SEAL)? The entry path flows from that answer.

### Step 2: Can military service pay for a BS or MS in engineering afterward? Yes — three mechanisms.

This meaningfully changes the cost math for Option 3 (Hobart Applied Physics) and for anyone who enters without an engineering BS.

1. **Post-9/11 GI Bill (after 3 years of active duty, 100% benefit).** Pays **$29,920.95/year for private schools** (2025–26 rate; adjusts annually) and **100% of in-state tuition at public schools**. Plus a monthly housing allowance (~BAH E-5 with dependents for the school's ZIP code) and $1,000/year for books. Covers up to 36 months of full-time enrollment. **Transferable to a spouse or dependents after 6 years of service with a 4-year additional commitment.**
2. **Yellow Ribbon Program** (private-school top-up to the GI Bill).
    - **Dartmouth:** uncapped — covers full remaining tuition, unlimited participants (A+ rating).
    - **Cornell:** covers the remainder, capped at ~100 veterans on campus, first-come-first-served for grad students.
    - **Columbia:** ~$8,000/year additional. Most veterans enter via the General Studies program; grad school (SEAS, etc.) participation varies.
    - **Stanford** (for reference): $16K–$20K additional for graduate engineering, uncapped.
    - **Practical implication:** **a veteran can attend Dartmouth Thayer, Cornell Engineering, or Stanford Engineering for near-zero tuition** if they have 100% GI Bill eligibility.
3. **Navy-funded graduate education (active duty).** While still in service, officers can apply for:
    - **CIVINS / Fleet Scholars Education Program (FSEP):** Navy pays full tuition + salary for up to 24 months at any accredited U.S. grad school (MIT, Stanford, Purdue, etc.) for a master's. Competitive, typically O-3 / O-4 officers with strong records.
    - **Naval Postgraduate School (NPS):** Monterey, CA. Navy-internal grad school offering master's in engineering fields, tuition-free for selected officers. Another standard path.
    - **Tuition Assistance (TA):** $4,500/year cap — useful for part-time coursework, not a full degree funder.

**Net cost implication for the military pathway:**

- If Jack enters as an officer and serves 3+ years, he can effectively get a **BS or MS at a top engineering school for $0 tuition** via GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon at Dartmouth (the most generous Ivy).
- If he makes O-3 / O-4 and gets selected for CIVINS, **a fully-funded master's at Stanford / MIT / Purdue while being paid** is a real possibility.
- Total tuition value of these benefits: **$120K–$300K+ depending on school and path**, before counting salary.

### Step 3: Entry paths (how he gets in)

#### (a) USNA this summer (if Jack can secure a nomination)

- **Reality check:** USNA does not accept transfer credit. If admitted, Jack **restarts as a plebe** — his Hobart freshman year is essentially lost academically (though not developmentally). He'd be Class of 2031 instead of Class of 2030.
- **Admit rate:** ~8%, plus the nomination hurdle from a senator, representative, or the VP.
- **Timing:** Application window typically opens April with a January 31 deadline; nominations require parallel tracks started in the spring.
- **Engineering:** USNA is an ABET-accredited engineering school; ~60% of midshipmen major in STEM. **Gets him a BS in engineering with no tuition cost and a 5-year active-duty commitment.**
- **Rowing:** Navy has a strong heavyweight program. Navy men's lightweight competes at Eastern Sprints; smaller roster than Harvard/Columbia/Dartmouth but legitimate DI lightweight competition.
- **Role implication:** USNA funnels into most designators (SWO, aviation, submarine, USMC, NUPOC-equivalent). Good if Jack is open across role types.
- **Risk:** Very high on the admit gate, very low on the "will he get a BS in engineering" question once admitted. The 5-year service commitment is a real life choice, not just a degree plan.

#### (b) NUPOC — junior or senior year of college

- **Eligibility:** calc-based physics, calc through DiffEq, ~3.0+ GPA (higher for NR Engineer at ~3.5+). **Physics majors are a standard NUPOC profile** — Hobart Applied Physics qualifies.
- **Benefits while in school:** $5,000+/month stipend, up to $38K signing bonus, total up to ~$168K before graduation.
- **Service:** 5 years active + 3 years inactive reserve.
- **Role:** Submarine officer, SWO-Nuclear, or Naval Reactors Engineer.
- **Best fit:** If Jack is drawn to the technical / nuclear role.

#### (c) OCS after graduation — any bachelor's degree

- **Unrestricted line (SWO, aviation, NFO, intel, supply):** requires a bachelor's in any field. Physics BA qualifies. Jack would commission as a line officer, 4-year commitment.
- **Engineering designator (EDO, CEC, Meteorology/Oceanography):** requires ABET-EAC engineering bachelor's. **Physics BA does NOT qualify.** WPI, UBC, Columbia/Dartmouth/WashU/RPI engineering BS all qualify.
- **Best fit:** If Jack wants operational leadership (SWO, aviation) or real engineering practice (CEC, EDO) — and has the right degree.

#### (d) Army Direct Commissioning — Engineer Officer (post-grad)

- **Requires:** ABET engineering BS + 3 years of relevant work experience. Longer term option — not a path at age 22, but a path by ~25–26.
- **Useful to know exists** — it's a second-chance commission track if Navy OCS doesn't work out.

#### ROTC — explicitly excluded

ROTC (NROTC / AROTC / AFROTC) was considered and **ruled out for capacity reasons.** Engineering coursework plus serious rowing is already a full academic + athletic load; layering ROTC's 0400 PT, weekly drill, summer training, and service obligations on top would compromise both GPA and rowing performance. The right military entry paths for Jack are **post-grad OCS/NUPOC** (applied for junior or senior year) or **USNA** (freshman-restart). ROTC is not on the table.

### How the military option interacts with each primary path

| Primary path | USNA this summer (replaces path) | OCS/NUPOC after (adds to path) |
|---|---|---|
| 1a. Hobart → Columbia | Giving up the 3:2 entirely to restart as a plebe | Columbia SEAS BS qualifies for **any designator** — all role types available |
| 1b. Hobart → Dartmouth | Giving up the 2:1:1:1 entirely to restart as a plebe | Dartmouth Thayer BE qualifies for **any designator** — all role types available |
| 2a. Hobart → WashU 3:3 | Same | Engineering BS qualifies — all doors open |
| 2b. Hobart → RPI 3:2 | Same | Engineering BS qualifies — all doors open |
| 3. Hobart Applied Physics only | Same | **NUPOC is a very strong fit here**; unrestricted-line OCS also works. **Does not qualify for engineering-designator OCS (EDO, CEC).** **Materially upgrades this option's end-state.** **GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon at Dartmouth can add a tuition-free MS in engineering post-service.** |
| 4. WPI outright | Possible | Engineering BS qualifies for all designators including CEC/EDO. Post-grad OCS is the route, not ROTC during college. |
| 6. UBC | USNA requires US citizen (Jack qualifies), but applying from Canada is logistically awkward | OCS possible; **NUPOC requires a regionally accredited U.S. school — UBC's CEAB-accredited BASc may not qualify.** Confirm with Navy recruiter if this path matters. |

### Practical recommendation on the military option

- **First: get clarity from Jack on what role appeals to him.** Is it nuclear/technical (→ NUPOC), operational leadership (→ SWO/aviation), engineering in uniform (→ CEC/EDO), or SpecOps/combat (→ USMC/SEAL)? This should be a conversation, not a research task.
- **Pursue USNA application this spring/summer in parallel** with whatever primary choice is made. It costs ~$0 beyond nomination legwork and the application effort. Rejection doesn't close any door; acceptance is a meaningful fork Jack can evaluate with actual admission in hand.
- **NUPOC doesn't require any action now** — it's a post-graduation option (applications typically submitted junior/senior year), and the eligibility windows extend up to 42 months past bachelor's completion. It just needs to stay on the radar as the end-state safety net for Option 3.
- **Treat post-service GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon as a real part of the cost math.** A common pattern: BS somewhere affordable → commission → serve 3–5 years → **use GI Bill + Yellow Ribbon for a Dartmouth Thayer / Cornell / Stanford MS in engineering at near-zero tuition**. This is especially relevant for Options 3, 4, and 6, where the undergrad credential is less prestigious but can be topped with a near-free elite MS.
